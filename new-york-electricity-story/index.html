Hot Days
The first week of July is extremely hot this year with highest temperature over 89.6°F (32°C).
The load in New York State goes up dramatically comparing to that of cooler days. From a broader view, the trend of the load increase has already begun in June.
Chilly Days
Apart from hot days, chilly days in winter are also potential high consumers.
Here're some days in the beginning of 2018 with temperature under 23°F (-5°C). The increasing tendency of electricity consumption gradually revealed at the end of 2017.
Christmas
On Christmas Eve and Christmas Day in 2017, the electricity load in New York State didn't stand out.
Instead of working on time, everyone was enjoying their wonderful family reunion and leisurable holidays.
Super Bowl !
Super Bowl LII was held on February 4, 2018, at U.S. Bank Stadium in Minneapolis, Minnesota.
The National Football Conference (NFC) champion Philadelphia Eagles defeated the American Football Conference (AFC) and defending Super Bowl LI champion New England Patriots, 41–33, to win their first Super Bowl and their first NFL title since 1960.
But exciting football games didn't bring a relatively high electricity load.
Weekends
These are the weekends in August, 2018. Comparing to nearby weekdays, it seems that they consumed less electricity.
While Westside never closes, most companies have a 2-day off to take a break.
In this way, lovely weekends may oppositely incur a lower electricity load.

Since the temperature has the biggest visual effect on the electricity load, we're now trying to use temperature as the x-axis to separate the factor and see more details.
Hover on one of the labels, we can see corresponding data in a highlighted form.
Focusing on temperature only, we can find out that both high temperature and low temperature have a positive relation with the electricity load. What's more, a high-temperature situation stands out more significantly.
Focusing on weekends and normal days (weekdays without other conditions), it's clearly illustrated that weekends mostly distribute below the normal days' boundary despite the temperature factor.
The short rest for people and their companies contributes to the reduction on electricity usage.
While answering the 'What' question, part of the answer to 'How' is also revealed.
Both natural(temperature) and human(weekends) factors contribute to the abnormality in electricity consumption. For the temperature factor, the electricity usage tends to go higher when it gets closer to either end of its extremes(cold or hot).

Generally, from the previous part, we notice that the electricity usage tends to go higher when either the temperature goes higher or lower.
What's more, it's not hard to find out here that hot or cold days share different patterns in hourly distribution.
For summer time, the peak hour appears around 3p.m. which is the hottest working hour.
For winter time, the peak hours appear around 12p.m. and 6p.m. which are near the lunch and the dinner time.

Historical Events
Finally, there're also some interesting historical events to be explored.
We extract data of the date when the certain event happened (highlighted), along with the data from previous day, following day and corresponding days in 2018.
the 58th Presidential Inauguration
(Human Factor)
Jan 19-21, 2017
Jan 19-21, 2018
Trump officially took office on this day.
It looks like the electricity load exceeds that of 2018. But, don't forget to look at the day of week. While Jan 20 in 2017 is Friday, the Jan 20 in 2018 is Saturday. A more probable explanation here would be a Weekends Effect.
the 2008 Global Financial Crisis
(Human Factor)
Sep 14-16, 2008
Sep 14-16, 2018
The case with the financial services firm Lehman Brothers on September 15, 2008, remains the largest bankruptcy filing in U.S. history, with Lehman holding over US$600,000,000,000 in assets.
While the electricity load seems to be higher than the nearby dates, we can't draw a clear conclusion here because lots of factors like weather, temperature and more factors from the financial perspective should be considered in this case.
Big Storm in 2017
(Natural Factor)
Mar 13-15, 2017
Mar 13-15, 2018
A Nor'easter moved up the eastern US coast on March 13th to late on the 14th. Heavy snow spread across parts of central New York and Pennsylvania late on March 13th. By late evening on the 14th snowfall amounts range from 8 to 33 inches of snow.
On March 15, the average temperature went down to 22°F (comparing to 28°F on March 13 and 14).
In this case, the Temperature Effect works, and together with the snowfall, the electricity load went up.
Human vs Nature
While there's no winner in electricity consumption, we should be aware of the change in electricity patterns and try our best to save energy. Moreover, it will help establishing a wiser electricity policy based on these patterns.
